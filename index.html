⚠ Historical Case: How Peter Williams Stole $50M Worth of US Gov Exploits

Peter Williams, 39, Australian national, former General Manager at Trenchant (L3Harris subsidiary). Over 3 years (2022-2025), he stole 8 zero-day exploits built exclusively for US government use. He sold them for $1.3M in cryptocurrency to Operation Zero (the sanctioned Russian broker). The Justice Department estimated $35M in losses. Williams was sentenced to 87 months federal prison. The stolen exploits end up being used against "numerous unsuspecting victims" according to prosecutors. This case demonstrates the real-world pipeline: US defense contractor → insider theft → Russian broker → offensive operations against US interests.

Most zero-day exploit transactions use cryptocurrency (BTC/XMR) and encrypted communications (Signal/Session/Wire). Dark web forum accounts typically require vouches from existing members and have limited registration periods.

Critical Context

The Cyber Arms Race

Understanding the dynamics of this ecosystem.

44% Annual Inflation

Zero-day exploit prices have been inflating at 44% per year for a decade. A $1M exploit in 2015 costs $7M+ today. Driven by platform hardening (Apple/Google security improvements) and nation-state demand outstripping supply.

Zero-Click is King

Exploits requiring user interaction (one-click) lose 60-80% of value. Zero-click exploits that work against iMessage/WhatsApp without any target action command the highest premiums. Physical-access exploits are discounted heavily.

Shelf Life Collapse

High-value exploits now have median shelf life under 90 days (2025). Vendors patch faster. Brokers are shifting to subscription-based exploit services. A zero-day purchased today may be worthless in 3 months.

The VEP Failure

US Vulnerability Equities Process (VEP) — NSA disclosed only 58% of vulnerabilities acquired in 2024. 42% were retained for offensive use. When the NSA retains exploits instead of disclosing, those exploits remain in the wild, available for theft, leakage, or sale.
